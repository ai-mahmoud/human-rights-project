The Petition of Right (1628)

Historical Context

The Petition of Right is a major English constitutional document that sets out specific liberties of the subject that the king is prohibited from infringing. Passed on 7 June 1628, the Petition contains restrictions on non-parliamentary taxation, forced billeting of soldiers, imprisonment without cause, and the use of martial law.

It was produced by the English Parliament in the run-up to the English Civil War. Following a series of disputes with King Charles I over finance and foreign policy, Parliament sought to curb the King's arbitrary use of power. The King had been forcing loans from his subjects and imprisoning those who refused to pay.

The Four Grievances

The Petition cited the Magna Carta and other statutes to argue that the King had violated the rights of his subjects in four key areas:

  • Taxation: No taxes could be levied without Parliament's consent. This challenged the King's "Divine Right" to rule as he saw fit.
  • Imprisonment: No subject could be imprisoned without a shown cause (reaffirming Habeas Corpus). This was a direct response to the "Five Knights' Case" where men were jailed for refusing the forced loan.
  • Billeting: No soldiers could be quartered upon the citizenry. This practice had been used to intimidate the population.
  • Martial Law: Martial law could not be used in peacetime.

Significance

The Petition of Right is regarded as one of England's most famous constitutional documents, of equal value to Magna Carta and the Bill of Rights 1689. It asserted the supremacy of the law over the personal will of the monarch and was a crucial step in the development of constitutional monarchy and the protection of civil liberties.
